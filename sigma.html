Σσς σίγμα / sigma /ˈsɪɡ.mə/

Sigma is a letter that holds an important place in both the Greek alphabet and modern technical fields. Its roots can be traced back to ancient Phoenicia. Greek scribes adopted this letter from the Phoenician alphabet, where it appeared as “shin.” That original character carried the meaning of a tooth. Early Greeks adapted its shape as they built their own system of writing. The transformation from a pictograph to a letter marks a clear change from a visual symbol to a building block of language.

The shape of sigma evolved over time. In early inscriptions, its form was more angular. As time went on, the Greeks introduced two variants for sigma. The letter appears in one form when it starts or is in the middle of a word and takes on a different shape at the end of a word. Nobody knows exactly why the two forms became necessary. Perhaps the change provided a visual cue that the letter was concluding a word.

Today sigma is more than just a letter in a classical alphabet. In mathematics, the capital sigma represents summation and helps students and professionals add up series of numbers. The lower-case sigma is used in statistics to denote standard deviation. Scientists and engineers also use sigma in various formulas. The letter appears in academic texts and computer programming notation. Sigma has made its mark in cultural contexts as well, appearing in logos and emblems of modern organisations.
