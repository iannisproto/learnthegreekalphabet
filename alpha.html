Αα άλφα / alpha /ˈæl.fə/

The Alpha letter, represented by the capital "Α" and lowercase "α," is the first alphabet letter in Greek. The letter was adapted from Phoenician "Aleph," which represented the ox and was used as the symbol for leadership and authority. The Greeks adopted the letter some time in the 8th century BCE and adjusted the shape and sound of the letter to accommodate their language.

Alpha in ancient Greece was greatly culturally esteemed. It was not just a letter; it signified beginnings. "Alpha and Omega," or beginning and end, is often used in literature and religion to signify completeness. Alpha is often used in science and mathematics to signify the initial in a sequence or the highest order, as it was once a chieftain.

Alpha is important today, too. In Greek, it is the start, that is to say that it comes first. In other contexts, such as business and academia, "alpha" is used to denote a measurement of performance or distinction, again referencing its continuing importance.

Apart from its numeric and cultural importance, Alpha has also entered popular mass culture as a surname. It is used in an alternative group of names, organisations, and companies implying something superior or quality. University fraternity and sorority clubs typically use Greek letters for the titles of their clubs, one of which is Alpha, as an indication that the club is the first or the best.
