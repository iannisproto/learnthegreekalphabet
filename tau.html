Ττ ταυ / tau /taʊ/

Tau is a letter with deep roots in ancient alphabets. Its story begins in the Phoenician era, where its precursor was known as “taw.” This word meant a mark or a sign, serving as a basic tool for communication in early civilisations. The Phoenicians carved tau in stone and clay, giving it a simple yet enduring design that influenced many future scripts.

The Greeks later adopted tau, modifying its shape to suit the phonetic needs of their language. In Greek, tau came to represent the [t] sound. Unlike some letters that attracted much attention, tau maintained a modest profile. Its straightforward appearance and practical role helped it become a reliable part of the Greek writing system.

As cultures mingled, tau passed on to the Etruscans and then to the Romans. The Roman version of tau is very similar to the Greek form, though minor stylistic changes mark its evolution. These changes did not alter the essential role of the letter; it remained a tool for everyday writing and communication. Nobody can dispute tau’s practical value even though it rarely grabs the spotlight like some of its alphabetical companions.
