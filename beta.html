Ββ βήτα / beta /ˈbiː.tə/

Beta is the Greek letter B. It was derived from the Phoenician letter "Beth," which was "house." The symbols of ancient alphabets generally looked like things, so they gave this letter the shape of a rough sketch of a house. When the Greeks adopted it, they altered the way it was pronounced to signify the /b/ sound. Its name, "beta," was merely a slight variation of its Phoenician origin.

Because the second letter in the alphabet, beta is traditionally used to stand for the digit two or that something is in the second in order. Conversely, compared to alpha, with all the pride of being at the front end, beta will typically be associated with being a second best. This has led to some modern contexts where "beta" is used to tag as secondary or second-rate, as something not necessarily praising.

The form of beta has changed through time. In early Greek inscriptions, it appeared more angular and different according to the style of writing. As the Greek alphabet grew more standardised, the form of beta settled into the form recognised today. When the Romans adopted the Greek alphabet into Latin, they modified beta somewhat and converted it into the letter B that is used today in numerous languages around the world.

Beta is also used extensively in science and mathematics. In physics, it denotes certain types of particles, while in finance it measures risk. It is also utilised in programming to refer to pre-final program versions before their completion.
