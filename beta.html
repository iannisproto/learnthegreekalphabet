Ββ βήτα / beta /ˈbiː.tə/

The letter Beta is the second letter of the Greek alphabet, represented by the capital "Β" and lowercase "β." It is derived from the Phoenician letter "Beth," which means "house" and symbolises a sense of belonging. The Greeks adopted this letter around the 8th century BCE, adjusting its form and sound to fit their language.

In ancient Greece, Beta was significant culturally. It was not just a letter. It represented the idea of following or supporting. The term "beta version" is commonly used today to describe a preliminary stage of development, reflecting its position as the second in a sequence. This notion of support and progression has made Beta a relevant symbol in various contexts.

Beyond its numeric and cultural significance, Beta has also made its way into popular culture. It appears in various names, organisations, and brands, often implying a secondary but essential role. in North American university life, many fraternities and sororities use Greek letters, and Beta is often chosen to signify a group that is second in a series or a supportive community.
