Εε έψιλον / epsilon /epˈsaɪ.lɒn/

The epsilon letter is from the Greek alphabet and originated from an extremely old writing system. It originated from the Phoenician alphabet where it was written as "he." The Phoenician term for "he" actually referred to "window." The Greeks adopted this letter and transformed it into epsilon, keeping the original sound but changing the shape slightly.

Epsilon was given the fifth place in the Greek alphabet. As Greek writing developed, the form of epsilon was made more elegant. In earlier varieties, it was something a little different, but over time the form was made more beautiful to what most are accustomed to viewing today. Its main purpose was to symbolise the "e" sound. Even though it seems like it was simply a simple mark on paper, this letter played an important role in the way words were formed and spoken in ancient writings.

In mathematics, it serves to represent a small value. You can find it in limit proofs and elsewhere where a small value will do. In science, epsilon can represent various constants and variables. Its purpose is basic and clear, much like the minimalist aesthetic.

Linguists use epsilon to describe vowel sounds as well. It is used to mark the manner in which a sound remains constant irrespective of the context in which it occurs. Fascinating that a character drawn thousands of years ago continues to play a part in high school courses today. No one knows if the original creators hoped the letter would be so useful. However, epsilon remains a small but important part of writing systems both in the past and in technical writing.
