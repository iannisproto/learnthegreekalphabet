Δδ δέλτα / delta /ˈdel.tə/

Delta is the fourth of the Greek letters. It's existed for thousands of years and has inspired writing systems and symbols in a number of societies. The letter originates from the Phoenician letter "dalet," used to signify "door." It was adopted by the Greeks, who gave it its contemporary triangle shape. It eventually found itself incorporated into the Latin alphabet and affected the letter D.

Delta has a great number of meanings. It is used to represent change in difference or value variations in mathematics and science. Delta is used quite often in equations that represent changes in increases and decreases in things like speed or temperature. It can be utilised to represent changes in the cost or demand of economics too. As a symbol for change, its use comes naturally because of the historical history it has built while moving from writing system to writing system.

In ancient Greece, the delta letter was also associated with physical architecture. Triangular land masses occurring at river mouths were called a "delta". This term survives to the present day, one of the best-known examples being the Nile Delta. The overlap between the delta letter's form and natural shapes is one of those things which is almost too good to be true.

Over time, delta came to represent structure and stability in some contexts, although it represented change in others. It appears in engineering, navigation, and even military terminology. Its geometric simplicity and triangular shape make it simple to recognise and replicate, which most likely explains its never-ending popularity.
