Ωω ωμέγα / omega /ˈəʊ.mɪ.ɡə/

Omega is the final letter in the Greek alphabet and has a rich history that starts with ancient Phoenician scripts. Its origin can be traced to the Phoenician letter "ʿayin", a word that means "eye." Early Greek speakers adopted this symbol to represent a long "o" sound, naming it Omega or "great O" to clearly distinguish it from Omicron, which stood for the short "o" sound. Nobody knew that a simple mark could spark such a practical naming scheme.

The Greeks altered the shape of this symbol as they moved from carving letters in stone to writing them by hand. The change was modest, addressing the need for fluidity in writing rather than a dramatic overhaul of its form. Omega kept its role as a marker for the long vowel sound while adapting to new writing tools and materials.

Today, Omega is used in many fields outside of language. In mathematics, it frequently represents the final element in a sequence or an unbounded set. In physics, the symbol stands for electrical resistance or angular velocity. Computer science also borrows Omega when discussing limits or ultimate values in algorithm analysis.
