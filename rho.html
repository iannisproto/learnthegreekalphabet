Ρρ ρώ / rho /rəʊ/

Rho has its origins in the ancient Phoenician alphabet. It comes from the Phoenician letter "resh", a term that meant “head.” Early on, a simple pictograph represented a head, and ancient traders adapted that symbol to stand for the /r/ sound. Greek scribes picked up the idea and gave us the familiar uppercase Ρ and lowercase ρ. Nobody knows why the Greeks made such slight modifications, but the Phoenician influence remains clear.

In Greek, rho became a standard character used in inscriptions and texts. Its shape stayed largely the same as writing methods evolved, even as its use expanded from everyday language to scholarly work. Greek mathematicians and scientists started to use ρ in their writings. In physics, for example, ρ is now used as a symbol for density. In mathematics, you might see it denoting correlation coefficients or other variables.

As the Latin alphabet emerged, many Greek letters were replaced for common language use, yet rho’s influence carried on in specialised fields. Even though modern writers favour the letter R for everyday use, ρ still appears in textbooks, research papers, and programming codes.
