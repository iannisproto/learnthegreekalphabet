Γγ γάμμα / gamma /ˈɡæm.ə/

Gamma is the third letter of the Greek alphabet. It is named after the Phoenician letter gimel, which originally meant "camel." Why a camel? Nobody knows, but ancient writing was riddled with random animal forms, so it probably seemed like a good idea at the time. When the Greeks adopted it, they changed its form and gave it a harder sound, similar to the English "g."

Gamma has all sorts of applications. In science, it refers to gamma rays, waves that are very high in energy and can pass through almost anything. It's also used in mathematics and physics to perform many calculations, as if the Greek alphabet wasn't already overused in those fields enough. In linguistics, gamma is used in pronunciation dictionaries, usually to confuse aspiring Greek language learners.

Its shape has varied, but it has never had a plain, angular shape. The earliest shapes were an upside-down "L," but the modern Greek lowercase shape (γ) is a nice, rounded one. When the Romans borrowed the Greek alphabet, they weren't entirely sure what to do with gamma, so they constructed it from the letter C, which is why "C" sometimes gives a hard sound for "G.".

While gamma is the third letter, somehow it is everywhere. It is in science, maths, statistics, and even ranking where one feels an urge to label things with Greek letters. It's not nearly as popular as alpha or beta, but people still use it anyway.
