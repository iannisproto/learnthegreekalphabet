Κκ κάππα / kappa /ˈkæp.ə/

The letter Kappa is the tenth character in the Greek alphabet and represents the /k/ sound. It comes from the Phoenician letter Kaph, a symbol originally linked to the word meaning "palm." Early Greek writers adopted Kappa and gave it a simple form that matched the needs of their language. Greek scribes used the letter in everyday inscriptions and more formal texts, and its design varied slightly from one region to another.

Over time, Kappa’s form became more uniform as writing practices evolved. In ancient Greece, many local variations existed, but the need for clear communication led to a standardized shape. As book manuscripts and later printed texts emerged, the letter was further refined to ensure legibility and ease of writing.

In addition to its phonetic use, Kappa played a part in the Greek numeral system by representing a specific value in some contexts. This practical use helped scholars and traders deal with numbers directly in their texts. Today, the letter is not only a part of modern Greek writing but also appears in academic texts across various fields. In mathematics and science, Kappa is used as a variable or a constant in equations.

Kappa’s form influenced the development of similar characters in other alphabets, including the Latin K. Its clear and unadorned structure has made it a consistent tool in written communication for centuries. The letter maintains its practical role by providing a direct and reliable symbol for the /k/ sound in both historical and contemporary contexts.
